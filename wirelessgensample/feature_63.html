Too Many Bones

Finding the first Mapusaurus bone was cool

Another big discovery gave Coria surprising new evidence. Searching again in the desert in Argentina, he found another new species of dinosaur. He named this species Mapusaurus (MAP-uh- SORE-us). Mapusaurus was a kind of meat-eating theropod, just like Giganotosaurus. Mapusaurus was also bigger than Tyrannosaurus rex!

In the spot where they were digging, Coria’s team uncovered too many bones to come from just one dinosaur. For example, they found one upper leg bone, then another, then a third—and parts of six more upper leg bones! Dinosaurs did not have nine legs, so Coria could infer that he was finding bones from more than one dinosaur in the same place.

From the shape of the bones, Coria could infer that they were all Mapusaurus bones. Coria and his team found bones from at least nine different Mapusaurus in that one spot.

Coria knew it was possible that the Mapusaurs had died at different times and their bones just happened to end up in the same place. He thought this was very unlikely, though. There were many more sauropods and other plant-eating dinosaurs around than big, meat-eating dinosaurs like Mapusaurus. If the Mapusaurs died at different times and just happened to be in that spot, why weren’t there any other kinds of dinosaurs there?

Coria inferred that the Mapusaurus fossils were all in the same place because lots of Mapusaurs had died together, at the same time. He inferred that they died together because they lived together. He inferred that this was a group of Mapusaurs— a family. Coria thought they may have died in a flash flood.

Coria investigated the fossil Mapusaurus bones. He compared the bones and found that some leg bones were smaller than others. He found that some of the teeth were much smaller than others. This was evidence that some of these Mapusaurus fossils were from young dinosaurs. He inferred that groups of Mapusaurs, including adult dinosaurs and young dinosaurs, lived together.

Coria says, “Finding the first Mapusaurus bone was cool. It was big and belonged to a huge, meat-eating dinosaur. But finding the second Mapusaurus bone was shocking, because it belonged to a teenaged Mapusaurus. I not only had found a new, big, meat-eating dinosaur, I also had found part of a family!”

After studying all of the fossils carefully, Coria had enough evidence to make an explanation. His claim was that Mapusaurs lived in groups. His evidence came from the group of Mapusaurus fossils he had found.

Explanation

Claim

*Mapusaurus* may have lived in groups.

Evidence

  • Lots of Mapusaurus bones were found in one place. There are too many bones to have come from just one dinosaur.
  • The bones are different sizes, which is evidence that the Mapusaurs were different ages when they died.
  • Family groups of animals usually include animals of different ages: adult animals and young animals.
  • No fossils from other species of dinosaurs were found in that spot, so it is unlikely that all those Mapusaurs died at different times and just happened to die in the same place. They probably all died together in a group.
