Q&A: Interview with Bart McWilliams, Pork Butcher

Bart McWilliams talks to Edith Gorman about short ribs and smokey marinades

Words
Edith Gorman

Bart McWilliams' butcher shop at number four Stump Street has been in the family for over fifty years. He talks with staff writer Edith Gorman about the changing face of local butchers and some of his favorite cuts.

Edith Gorman - You spent time in your father's butcher shop as a kid. Did you always know you wanted to follow in his footsteps?

Bart McWilliams - Basically yes! My sister Lydia and I loved spending time here as kids, and by the time I was old enough for a part-time job, I was making deliveries, meeting farmers, learning about the different cuts, you name it. Working at the shop helped me to put myself through high school and college.

EG - How has the market changed since you started running the shop?

BMcW - People care more now about where their food comes from. Even though many folks have less disposable money now, they are more careful about how they spend it. They want to know that the meat is clean and uncompromised, and they're willing to pay more for sustainably farmed food. This is really encouraging and a great trend to see.

I also notice that people are more savvy about the different cuts. When I was a teenager, there were steaks, chops and roasts, but now people know the difference between a flank steak and a hanger steak, a short rib and a thick rib, a chuck roast and a rump roast. They're willing to talk you through what they're cooking and take suggestions, too.

EG - What are some frequent suggestions you give?

BMcW - A common mistake with steak is cooking it while it's still cold from the fridge. Great steak cooks even better if it's at room temperature, so I always encourage people to leave it out for a bit before cooking.

Another suggestion is to lightly sear both sides of the meat before cooking it through. This helps immensely to seal in the juices and keep it from drying out.

EG - What cuts are the least popular?

BMcW - It's surprising to me how regional these things are. Oxtail and pork trotters are quite popular in Ireland and the UK, but not here in Missouri. Liver can be quite trendy; right now it's popular with those following the Paleo diet, so we sell a lot of veal and lamb liver as well as pork liver.

EG - If someone wanted to learn more about working with different meats and cooking them, what would you suggest?

BMcW - Well, working in a butcher shop is great if you can do it. If someone is looking for more of a course or a one-off lesson, I would recommend two things: 1. Ask around at your local gourmet food shops and butcher shops too for classes like that. People who work at places like Williams-Sonoma always seem to have good recommendations. Cooking courses as part of travel are also becoming quite popular, one of my customers did a "Pig in a Day" course as part of her recent trip to Wales and loved it. 2. Just jump in. Afraid of a certain type of meat? Go ahead and buy it. Find a good butcher, tell him or her what you want to try and ask for suggestions. Chances are they'll give you a nice, easy cut and some solid advice for making it in a tasty, appealing way.

If it doesn't work out or taste nice? Try again. That's how we all learn, and trying it is truly the joy of cooking.
