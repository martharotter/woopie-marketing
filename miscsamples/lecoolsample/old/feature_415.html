Le Cool Guides

KMZERO

Le Cool gets cosy with Studio KmZero, the experimental Florentine design house that mixes graphic design, typography, new media and digital art.

How would you describe Kmzero and your work there?

We met 12 years ago while working in the same big agency in Florence. It was fun living in the new economic bubble, until someone decided to crash a jet liner into a skyscraper, and the first of many crises hit us. There were five of us at the time: a couple of comic artists, a game designer, a designer with kung fu skills and a redhead. We could either create a group of superheroes or open a design studio, so we chose to do both. We’ve tried to keep the same spirit since the beginning: work hard, do interesting stuff, be nice to others. We called this philosophy “design positive”.

What makes good design, in your opinion?

One could answer easily by quoting Dieter Rams’ principles, but we learned that in the end, good design is made mostly by the right combination of a good designer and a good client. Good design is the product of a relationship that, like any other, needs a lot of mutual respect and commitment. We have a strong heritage of design in Italy. The modernist school of the 1950’s/60’s had a profound influence on the following generations, and many young designers still have a quasi-mystical respect for designer guru Bruno Munari, who embodied all the best aspects of Italian design spirit: playful approach, technical knowledge, flexibility of mind and curiosity without limits.

Who are your favourite Italian artists?

We’ve had too many great artists in the past to make choices, so let’s stick to contemporary influences! Maurizio Cattelan is really worth watching: all his work is inspiring and brilliant. Manuele Fior is one of our finest graphic novelists, and his work is a pleasure for the eyes. Alessandro Baricco is a great Italian writer, Paolo Sorrentino is probably the best Italian movie director around. We personally know our favourite Italian photographer (Gabriele Galimberti) and our favourite Italian illustrator (Ilaria Falorsi) works in our studio.

What does being Italian mean to you?

Every stereotype you can think of applies to us. Better food, lazy and noisy, messy and friendly. And yes, we do it better.
