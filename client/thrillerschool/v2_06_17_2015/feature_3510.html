Crime Fiction Fix

Ways with words



 

Whose voice is it anyway?

One of the most difficult things to do as a writer is to establish your characters’ voices. By this I don’t just mean how they speak and what kind of language they use, but how they think and see the world.

If you are writing as a third person narrator – "Jane walked into the room and saw the body" – which is by far the most usual and straightforward way of constructing a narrative, then it is easy to get seduced into giving all your characters’ thoughts the same "voice"– your voice. But different people will have different thoughts and react differently, even in the same situation.

So let’s play a bit.

First of all, let’s make Jane a young girl, say thirteen or fourteen years old, and give a little context to her thoughts and feelings. "Jane had run all the way home. She’d promised Rhianna to IM her the moment she got home so that they could go on chatting about that new boy in Mr. Lanyon’s class. She threw open the living room door and saw a crumpled figure lying face down on the floor. The first thing she thought was that her mom had had another fall. She took a step then stopped dead. It wasn’t her mom, it was a stranger, and she looked all wrong."

Compare this with an alternative narrative voice, this time where Jane is a private eye. "Jane pushed the door open cautiously. It was quiet, too quiet. Her hand went instinctively to where her baton used to hang, but she had had to hand it in along with the rest of her uniform. Oh well, too late now. Her eyes surveyed the room as the door swung open. No movement. A woman, prone. Nasty amounts of blood."

In each case, as well as there being a different scenario, Jane is thinking in a different way, using different language, because of her different positions in life, and her different frames of reference. And we learn about what it is like to be her by experiencing how she sees the world.

Why don’t you have a go at creating two or three different character voices, using the basic building blocks of Jane and a body, and see what you come up with? If you’d like to send them in to us, then we’ll put the best ones up in the August issue.
