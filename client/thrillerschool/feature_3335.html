Crime Fiction Fix

Members' showcase

'The Fox' by Stephen Davis

It had been a profitable two days, thought James Rowland. He had no regrets about changing his job. Unlike his previous work, there had been no interview, no qualifications were needed and no boss to satisfy. Crime was proving to be exciting and would soon be profitable and he was only sorry he hadn’t made the change sooner.

Cradled in the soft leather seat of his sleek and powerful Jaguar XK, Rowland, as he introduced himself to strangers, pushed down on the accelerator and the speedo rose as he raced along the A420 towards the motorway. He liked driving the sports car. It wasn’t particularly sunny but nevertheless he wore the Oakley sunglasses he felt completed the image he desired. As the DJ introduced Shirley Bassey singing ‘Diamonds are for ever’, he turned up the volume on the quadrophonic music system so that she could do justice to the song. The hand on the speedo rose further.

He’d liked the other participants at Thriller School. They had been fun and a few had written hugely successful crime-fiction books. He’d enjoyed the two days talking about writing plots, characterisation, how to create cliff-hanger endings to a chapter and marketing the novel so that it became a bestseller. In his opinion the weakness of the other authors was that they insisted the crime was always solved by some gentleman detective, private eye or Lincoln lawyer to create a satisfactory conclusion. His hero, the Fox, was different. He pictured the Fox as a ruthless assassin who never discriminated between good and evil but who, when hired, always completed the contract of removing a politician, banker, Head of State, redundant wife or business partner. The Fox was a master of his art who left the police baffled in Europe, America and the Far East.

There had been only one cloud that had spoilt the two days of Thriller School. That was the speaker on the second day. The Chief Inspector who’d insisted there was no such thing as ‘the perfect crime’. Rowland had listened as the detective had droned on about the databases, the DNA and the forensic archaeology that always ended up catching the killer. Rowland hadn’t liked the policeman from the moment he’d stood up. The balding head and self-satisfied smile had reminded him of his old boss, the boss who had made him redundant and had given him the news that he’d lost his job, that had led to him losing his house, and finally his marriage as if he was ticking off some minor task on the day’s to-do list.

As Rowland sped past a white van he had an idea. He would prove the policeman wrong. The process of committing the perfect crime would give him valuable experience that he could use to develop the story and character of the Fox. It would make the Fox believable, the novel real and three dimensional. He would kill his old boss.

His boss rented one of the spacious apartments the Pickton-Turbeville family let out on their three thousand acre estate at Ewenny Priory in Glamorgan and each morning he began his day early by walking in the woods adjoining the pheasant shoot. If found lying dead, his face shredded by shotgun pellets, the South Wales police could only assume there had been a terrible accident.

Arriving home at his small house in Bridgend Rowland set about researching how the Fox might assassinate his target. A shotgun would be too messy and too loud. Instead his hero would use a self-built pneumatic air rifle. He would build the weapon as if he were his hero. It would be useful when writing his novel and would thus feel the words and emotions to describe the process.

Over the next few days Rowland recorded how the Fox researched how to assemble the weapon and weeks passed as the Fox experimented. He wanted to make sure he was getting a good, airtight seal on all parts. He learnt how long he had to wait for the glue to dry properly. Too early and caps popped off on parts and had the potential to be very dangerous.

Once the chamber was built the Fox tested it. He placed it in a bucket of water and filled it with compressed air to check for leaks. He was very careful. If the chamber blew up in a bucket of water, it would be a very big explosion. The chamber was good and airtight. He tested it up to a hundred pounds per square inch. He was good to move on.

The barrel was made of PVC. It was just a pipe eighteen inches long with a one inch thread so that the metal balls he would use as ammunition would fly straight. The Fox wanted to use a pistol grip for the handle so as to keep the weapon steady and bought a cheap airsoft gun from a toy store. Painting, of course, was a necessity to enhance the overall look of the device and he chose matt black. When finished the Fox looked at his work with satisfaction.

Before dawn on the day chosen by the Fox for the assassination of the man that had given Rowland the push - the Fox carefully dressed in army surplus camos and hid in the undergrowth waiting for his target. It was cold, uncomfortable and his legs had begun to cramp when the man came into sight. The target strolled along the path breathing in the morning air. The Fox, aimed and squeezed the trigger. A hiss of compressed air projected a stream of shotgun pellets that drilled themselves into the head of the target who fell to the ground dead. The Fox waited to see if anyone approached, all was quiet, only a pheasant scratched for food in the undergrowth. Five minutes later the Fox was driving away from the scene in the car, bought with the redundancy money. The Fox had completed another successful mission. Rowland now knew how to write into his novel the excitement and adrenalin rush he had felt as the trigger had been squeezed.

Back in the woods, a photographer emerged from the hide he’d built to film the lesser spotted woodpecker which had been observed nesting in the area a few days earlier. The photographs he’d taken over the previous half hour were not the ones he had expected he would shoot.


Philip Cummings hated change, but when his high-flying job at the Bank of England takes him to Russia he’s caught up in the chaos, turmoil, violence and vengeance of the Bolshevik Revolution. Philip must survive double-cross and cold-blooded murder if he’s to bring the beautiful Countess Sophie Tagleva, her wounded brother, a chest full of the Tsar’s jewels and secret papers out of Russia, and free them from the clutches of all the fighting factions swirling around them.

An exciting, fast-paced historical thriller, immaculately researched, this is the first in a series following Philip Cummings and his family through two world wars and beyond.
