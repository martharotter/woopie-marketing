Chapter Three: Creating Better Financial Habits

Budgeting isn’t just about following what you see on a sheet of paper - realistically sticking with it requires a bit more work than that. We’ve already talked about the psychological trick of giving yourself a spending allowance but another aspect of better budgeting will simply come from creating better habits. And what’s one of the most important habits of all? Paying yourself first.

For example, let’s say you’ve created a budget that looks great on paper. All your bills are accounted for and maybe you even have a little bit left over each month. Then you pay all your bills and live life for a month, only to realize that you don’t have enough money left to put into your savings account. How could this happen?

The fact is, even when you’re tracking your spending you can accidentally go over budget - either from unexpected expenses or simply spending more on something than you were supposed to. And if you see the money in your checking account, you might not think twice about it. That’s where paying yourself first comes in.

Why You Should Pay Yourself First

If you make a practice of paying yourself first, then you can pretty much assume that your savings account (or debt payoff plan) will continue on at the rate you planned. That’s because you’re moving that money out of your checking account and it’s as unavailable to you as the money you paid into your electrical bill.

Paying yourself first doesn’t just ensure that you stay on top of your savings goals. It also gives you a chance to show yourself that you deserve to save and that you can save. You’re prioritizing your future over your present - and that’s the mindset you’ll need to achieve financial success.

How You Can Pay Yourself First

It’s simple! Figure out when all of your bills are due and when you have enough money left to pay from your check to your savings. For example, you may need to do this with a paycheck later in the month if you have a lot of bills due out of your first paycheck of the month. Another option is to split it up between all of your paychecks in a month.

Whatever the case is, determine when and how often you will be paying out of your paycheck to your savings each month in order to achieve the savings goal you outlined. Then mark those dates on a calendar and make sure you put the designated amount into your savings account on those days.

Need a trick to help you remember? Automate it. Set up automatic deductions to go to your savings account straight from your direct deposit or have an automatic withdrawal from your checking to your savings each month. Then you can be sure you’ll never miss a date.
