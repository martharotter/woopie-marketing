Chapter Five: Managing Your Income

Once you’ve got the job, divert all that attention you used to finding the job to building a solid financial base for yourself. If you’re new to the workforce, the tips that follow will help you learn how to manage your finances with your newfound income. If you’re a seasoned veteran, you may just find some tips to help improve your current financial plan.

Getting Your Finances in Order Once You’ve Got the Job

1. Get Organized

The first thing you should do is get organized so you can have a good idea of your financial situation. What bills do you have to pay and what debt do you have? Consider things like cell phone, groceries, entertainment, student loans, credit cards, and so on. Then, calculate how much money you’ll be making after taxes and other deductions, as well as how often you’re going to get paid. If you’re not sure yet, go to websites like paycheckcity.com to get an estimate.

2. Create a Spending Plan

Now that you know how much you’ll likely be taking home each month, subtract that amount from your monthly bills. Do you have any money left over? If not, then it’s time to evaluate – what can you cut from your monthly expenses? If there’s nothing you can cut, is there anything you can reduce the frequency of (for example – going out to dinner once a month instead of every weekend)? The most important thing to do is make sure that you are covering all of your bills. If not, it might be time to look into getting a side job. If you have student loans, you can also look into government programs which can potentially reduce your payment.

What if you do have money left over? That’s great! Now let’s talk about assigning a job to that extra money. What are your immediate goals? Some things to think about are building an emergency fund of three months to one year of living expenses, start saving for retirement (the earlier the better), and paying off debt. Don’t forget to plan some funds for more fun goals too, such as travel or future home ownership.

3. Reduce Debt Faster

If you graduated with student loan and/or credit card debt, then it’s strongly advised that you apply any extra money to paying that debt off first. The fact is, debt costs money. While it’s not the end of the world if you have debt, it is prudent to focus on paying it down once you earn enough to do so. Once you’re debt-free, you’ll stop losing money to interest and have more money to apply to other goals.

Want some tricks to paying it down faster? One thing you could do is make biweekly payments on your debt. To do this, you would split your monthly minimum payment in half and then pay that amount every other week. Believe it or not, this can have a HUGE impact on your debt payoff. Plug your numbers into this calculator to see for yourself. After that, use a website like ReadyForZero to help you track and automate your debt payoff. The more you automate the process, the easier it will be for you to pay it all off.

4. Take Advantage of Benefits

Besides the ability to grow your career, the beauty of working your first professional job is having perks such as a 401(k) plan. A 401(k) plan is a retirement fund in which you can have a percentage of your paycheck automatically deducted (pre-tax) each pay period and deposited into your plan. Even better, your employer might offer to “match” your deduction. That means they deposit the same amount of money into your 401(k) – in other words, free money!

If you’re not sure if your new employer matches, talk to your HR contact to find out. If they do match, deduct the same amount they do. Not to do so is literally throwing money away. For example, if your employer matches up to 5% and you’re only deducting 3%, then you’re throwing away the equivalent of 2% of your paycheck which they would be giving to your 401(k). What you should do in that situation is deduct the whole 5% so they’ll match that amount. Yes, this is more money out of your pocket each month, but it’s well worth it!

This may seem like a lot to do while starting your first professional job, but once you take these steps, managing your finances should be a breeze moving forward. It’s all about developing new habits as soon as you’re able to earn more money and keep that momentum moving forward as you grow your career.

How to Earn Extra Income

Your new income may not be enough to cover your financial goals as quickly as you’d like. So if you need more income, how can you speed up the process without losing out on a job you’ve just worked so hard to acquire? Earn extra income on the side! In this day and age it’s easy to earn extra income without having a negative impact on your day. Here are a few tips to help:

1. Determine How Much Spare Time You Have

The first thing you want to do before trying to make more money is figure out how much spare time you have in the first place and then create a plan accordingly.

Once you determine how many hours during the week, at night, and on weekends you have to devote to increasing your income, you can start mapping out how to properly leverage those hours. The goal is to work smarter, not harder!

2. Know How Much Your Time is Worth

“Your time is worth $1,000/hour and you need to act accordingly”. ~ Jason Cohen

What that quote means is to not waste your time taking on jobs or projects that aren’t worth much. If you respect your time, it will pay you back.

Is it more beneficial to save time, save money, or make money? Well, if you’re spending a lot of time trying to save money and end up wasting 2 extra hours in the process, then that’s not a very smart use of your time.

The same goes for trying to land a lower paying job when you can get the same amount of pay for half the hours. It’s all about understanding yourself, your needs, and the value of your time.

3. Ask For a Raise or Increase Your Prices

While you’re learning to respect your time, one of the best things to do is ask for a raise (if you work for someone else) or increase your prices (if you work for yourself).

This is all part of working smarter not harder - and what could be better than making more money in the same amount of time? That’s the benefit of understanding what your time is worth.

4. Apply the Gigs for Goals Method

The “gigs for goals” idea is about giving your goals and your saving habits a new perspective. For instance, start by writing down a goal, like taking a summer vacation, and then find one new income stream that will pay for it.

Planning for an life event like a wedding or buying a house? Start selling stuff you no longer need online or via consignment shops.

Trying to max out your retirement savings? Start using your skills to make money or start a business from home and stash away your earnings into an IRA.

5. Leverage Your Assets

If you don’t have a lot of spare time to devote to a side-job, another smart idea is to rent out your current assets like your car or your home.

If you have a spare room or basement in your home, consider listing it on AirBnB. Renting out your home is also good for those occasions when you’re out of town. Why not bring in some extra income while you’re away?

The same goes for your car. If you live in a big city, you can make money renting out your car with RelayRides. And since real estate is a prized possession in most large cities, you can even rent out your parking spot thanks to ParkatMyHouse.

By applying these steps, you’ll be on your way to successfully increasing your income without being too overwhelmed by extra work. It’s just a matter of understanding the value of your time, getting creative, and choosing the right avenue for your lifestyle.

Retirement Savings Strategies for All Ages

Simple math suggests that the sooner you start saving for retirement, the better. Even putting away just a tiny bit of money each month now can help you save more than if you were to save double that amount ten years from now – all thanks to something called compound interest.
